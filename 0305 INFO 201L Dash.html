Big Picture Questions
Project Overview Big Picture Questions - How is sleep duration associated with stress level across individuals? - Are individuals with elevated heart rates more likely to report high stress levels?? - Does shorter sleep duration (less than 6 hours) predict higher stress levels?

Adequate sleep plays a critical role in both physical and psychological well-being. Research from the National Sleep Foundation indicates that insufficient sleep is associated with increased stress, impaired emotional regulation, and measurable changes in physiological functioning such as heart rate (https://www.sleepfoundation.org/stress-and-sleep). These findings suggest that sleep, stress, and cardiovascular activity are interconnected, making them important variables to examine together.

To investigate these relationships, we use two publicly available datasets from Kaggle: the Sleep Health and Lifestyle Dataset (https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/uom190346a/sleep-health-and-lifestyle-dataset) and the Human Stress Detection in and through Sleep Dataset (https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/laavanya/human-stress-detection-in-and-through-sleep). Both datasets include measures of sleep duration, heart rate, and stress-related indicators. By combining these datasets, we are able to analyze patterns across behavioral (sleep duration) and physiological (heart rate) variables in relation to reported stress levels.

Understanding these relationships is important because stress is linked to long-term health risks, including cardiovascular disease, weakened immune response, and reduced overall quality of life. Sleep duration is a modifiable behavior, and heart rate is increasingly monitored through wearable technology. By exploring how these factors interact, our analysis aims to provide insight into how everyday health behaviors and physiological signals may be connected.

There are also important ethical considerations and limitations. Some measures in the datasets rely on self-reported stress levels, which may introduce subjective bias. Additionally, the samples may not be fully representative of the broader population due to limited demographic detail. As a result, our findings should be interpreted as associations within the dataset rather than causal conclusions about all individuals.

How is sleep duration associated with stress level across individuals?
Scatterplot
Brief Explanation

This scatterplot displays the relationship between sleep duration (measured in hours) and reported stress level for individuals in the combined dataset. Each point represents one observation, showing a participant’s sleep duration and corresponding stress level. The blue regression line summarizes the overall linear trend in the data.

The downward slope of the regression line suggests a negative association between sleep duration and stress level. In general, individuals who report fewer hours of sleep tend to report higher stress levels, while those who sleep longer hours tend to report lower stress. However, variability in the data indicates that sleep duration is not the sole factor influencing stress levels.

Are individuals with elevated heart rates more likely to report high stress levels?
Bar Graph
Brief Explanation

This bar chart compares the proportion of individuals reporting high stress levels between those with elevated heart rates and those with lower heart rates. Heart rate was categorized into two groups based on whether it exceeded 75 beats per minute, and stress was categorized as “high” when reported stress levels met or exceeded our defined threshold. The height of each bar represents the proportion of individuals within each heart rate group who reported high stress.

The visualization suggests that individuals with elevated heart rates have a higher proportion of high stress compared to those with lower heart rates. This pattern indicates a positive association between elevated heart rate and reported stress levels. However, while the difference in proportions suggests that high heart rate is associated with greater likelihood of high stress, the data does not establish causation and may be influenced by additional physiological or behavioral factors.

Brief Explanation

This graph demonstrates relationship between stress level, sleep duration, and heart rate. The line represents the average stress level for each sleep duration and the dot represent heart rate values for each sleep duration and stress levels. From this graph, we can observe that shorter sleep duration (less than 6 hours) tend to have higher stress than lower sleep duration.

The ‘Average Stress Level Chart’ tab supports the observation by showing the mean stress levels for lower and higher sleep duration. In this chart, TRUE represents sleep duration more than 6 hours and FALSE represents less than 6 hours. We randomly choose 240 TRUE and FALSE value from column ‘High Stress.’ The result shows that average stress level for the FALSE is 7 and average stress level for TRUE is 3.

Based on this calculation, we can assume that shorter sleep duration is associated with higher stress levels.

Graph 1

For this visualization, we used geom_smooth(method = “lm”) to add a linear regression line to the scatterplot. This function fits a linear model directly within the plot, allowing us to visually assess the direction and strength of association between sleep duration and stress level. While we could have manually calculated regression coefficients using base R modeling functions, geom_smooth() allowed us to integrate the model directly into the visualization in a clear and interpretable way.

We also used the alpha argument in geom_point() to adjust transparency. Because the dataset contains many overlapping points, reducing opacity improves readability by preventing overplotting. These arguments were not heavily emphasized in class, so we referenced the official ggplot2 documentation and online visualization guides to better understand how to improve clarity in dense scatterplots.

Graph 2

For this analysis, we used mutate() to create logical variables identifying elevated heart rate and high stress. This conditional transformation allowed us to convert continuous variables into categories so we could compare proportions between groups more clearly.

We then applied group_by() and summarize() to calculate the proportion of high stress within each heart rate category. Using mean() on a logical variable efficiently computes proportions in R, which allowed us to quantify differences rather than rely only on visual interpretation. We used geom_col() instead of geom_bar() because our data was already summarized, and we confirmed this distinction using the ggplot2 documentation.

Graph 3

In the Graph 3, we used gganimate and virdis libraries, which were essential library to creating an animated graph. Since animated graphs were not covered in class, we searched external resources to learn how to create animated graph.

Three Major Takeaways

Our analysis reveals three key patterns across the combined datasets. First, sleep duration demonstrates a negative association with stress levels. Individuals reporting fewer hours of sleep tend to report higher stress, as reflected in the downward slope of the regression line in our scatterplot.

Second, elevated heart rate appears to be associated with a higher likelihood of reporting high stress, as shown by the increased proportion of high-stress cases among individuals with heart rates above 75 bpm.

Third, when examining sleep duration, stress level, and heart rate together, shorter sleep is often accompanied by both higher stress and elevated physiological activity, suggesting that behavioral and physiological measures are interconnected.

Over / Under / Bias

While these patterns are consistent across the dataset, our analysis may overestimate or underestimate the strength of these relationships due to sampling limitations. Both datasets rely partially on self-reported measures and wearable device data, which may introduce measurement error. Additionally, the datasets are not drawn from a nationally representative sample and may lack demographic diversity. Because we joined two separate datasets that may have different measurement scales and collection methods, some relationships may appear stronger or weaker than they truly are in the broader population.

Future Directions

Future research could strengthen this analysis by incorporating longitudinal data to observe how sleep and stress interact over time. Including demographic variables such as age, occupation, or geographic region would also allow for more nuanced subgroup analysis. Additionally, applying formal statistical modeling techniques, such as correlation coefficients or regression diagnostics, could provide more precise estimates of the strength of association between sleep duration, heart rate, and stress level. These steps would allow for a more robust understanding of how behavioral and physiological indicators interact in shaping overall well-being.
